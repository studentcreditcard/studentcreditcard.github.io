720 Credit Score: The Role of Authorized User Accounts

Home / Blog / Blog Details

In the labyrinthine world of personal finance, a number holds immense power: 720. This isn't just a score; it's a gateway. It’s the velvet rope separating you from premium interest rates, coveted rewards credit cards, and the nod of approval from mortgage lenders. For millions, hitting that 720 FICO score feels like a distant summit. But what if there was a secret path, a shortcut that has been both a family tradition and a topic of heated algorithmic debate? Enter the powerful, often misunderstood, strategy of becoming an Authorized User (AU) on a seasoned credit account.

This isn't just about adding a name to a card. In today's economic climate, characterized by soaring inflation, rising interest rates, and a looming shadow of potential recession, optimizing your credit health is not a luxury—it’s a necessity. For young adults burdened by student loan debt, immigrants building credit from scratch, or individuals rebuilding after a financial setback, the AU strategy presents a unique opportunity. However, it operates in a world where fintech lenders are using increasingly sophisticated AI models, and the rules of the game are constantly evolving.

What Exactly is an Authorized User Account?

Let's break it down. An authorized user is a person who receives permission to use someone else’s credit card account but is not legally responsible for paying the bill. The primary cardholder remains solely liable for all charges.

The Mechanics: How It Works

The primary cardholder simply requests from their card issuer to add another individual (e.g., a child, spouse, or close friend) to their account. The new user gets a card with their name on it. Crucially, many card issuers—though not all—will subsequently report the entire account’s history to the credit bureaus (Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion) under the authorized user’s credit profile.

This is the magic. Imagine your credit history is a photo album. If yours is empty or has a few unfortunate pictures (late payments, high utilization), becoming an AU allows you to splice in a beautiful, full-page, high-definition photo from someone else's album. You instantly inherit the age of that account, its payment history, and its credit limit, all of which are fundamental factors in calculating your FICO score.

The Primary Cardholder vs. The Authorized User

The distinction in responsibility is critical. The primary cardholder is the architect of the account. They built it, they maintain it, and their credit is on the line for every dollar spent. The authorized user is a privileged guest. They can enjoy the benefits of the house (the credit account) but aren’t on the hook for the mortgage. Their credit can benefit from the primary user's financial discipline, but it can also be damaged by their mistakes.

The Direct Impact on Your Journey to a 720 Score

A 720 credit score is universally classified as "good," bordering on "very good." It’s the sweet spot where lenders see you as a reliable, low-risk borrower. The AU strategy attacks several key components of your credit score to help you reach this plateau.

Boosting Your Credit Age and History

The length of your credit history accounts for about 15% of your FICO score. For a young person with a new credit card that’s only 6 months old, adding an AU account that’s 10 years old can dramatically increase their average age of accounts. This provides an immediate credibility boost that would otherwise take a decade to build.

Improving Credit Utilization Ratio

This is arguably the most powerful short-term lever in your credit score, making up 30% of the calculation. It’s the amount of credit you’re using divided by your total available credit. By being added to an account with a high credit limit and a low balance, you suddenly increase your total available credit. If you personally have a card with a $1,000 limit and a $500 balance, your utilization is a high 50%. If you’re added as an AU to an account with a $10,000 limit and a $1,000 balance, your overall utilization plummets. Your total available credit is now $11,000, and your total balances are $1,500, resulting in a healthy ~14% utilization ratio. This single action can cause a significant score jump.

Enhancing Payment History

The most important factor, at 35% of your score, is payment history. If the primary cardholder has a flawless, long-term record of on-time payments on the account, that positive history gets grafted onto your credit report. This is invaluable for someone with a thin file or a few past missteps. It acts as a counterbalance, showing potential lenders a consistent pattern of reliability.

Navigating the Pitfalls and Modern Considerations

The AU strategy is not a silver bullet without risks. Its effectiveness has been tempered by modern scoring models and the inherent need for trust.

The Algorithmic Arms Race: FICO 8 and Beyond

Credit scoring models are wise to potential manipulation. The FICO 8 score, the most widely used version today, and newer models like FICO 9 and 10, are designed to identify and potentially discount "piggybacking" that appears to be abusive (e.g., strangers buying and selling AU spots on ultra-aged accounts). The models look for a "natural" relationship between the primary user and the AU, such as a shared surname or address. While adding a family member still works powerfully, the era of commercially trading AU slots is largely over due to these smarter algorithms.

The Double-Edged Sword: Risk to the Authorized User

Your credit fate is tied to the primary cardholder’s financial habits. If they start maxing out the card, your utilization ratio skyrockets. If they miss a payment, that negative mark will likely appear on your credit report as well, causing severe damage. This requires an immense amount of trust. You must be absolutely confident in the primary user’s financial stability and responsibility.

The Trust Factor: A Two-Way Street

The risk isn’t one-sided. The primary cardholder is trusting the AU not to go on a spending spree. While they are not liable for the AU's charges, they are responsible for paying them. A reckless AU can create financial and relational strain. Clear rules on spending—or even cutting up the AU card immediately after receiving it to prevent any use—are common and prudent strategies to mitigate this risk.

Strategic Implementation: How to Use AU Accounts Wisely

To harness this tool effectively, you need a plan.

Choosing the Right Account

Not all accounts are created equal. The ideal AU account is: * Old: A long history is best. * In Good Standing: Impeccable on-time payment history. * High Credit Limit: To maximize the utilization benefit. * Low Balance: Consistently low utilization on that specific card. A maxed-out card from 1998 will hurt you more than it helps.

Having "The Talk"

This is a serious financial agreement. The primary user and AU must have a candid conversation about expectations, spending rules, and financial goals. It’s crucial to agree on whether the AU will ever use the card and, if so, how reimbursements will work.

Verification and Patience

After being added as an AU, it can take one or two billing cycles for the account to appear on your credit report. Not all issuers report AU activity to all bureaus, so it’s wise to check all three reports eventually to ensure the strategy is working as intended.

The path to a 720 credit score is a marathon, not a sprint. Authorized user accounts are not a cheat code, but they can be like a powerful energy gel that gives you a massive boost mid-race. They provide a proven method to build a thicker, healthier credit file faster. In an uncertain global economy, where access to affordable credit can be a lifeline, understanding and strategically utilizing every tool at your disposal is the mark of a savvy financial actor. By leveraging the trust and good credit of a family member responsibly, you can open doors to a more secure and prosperous financial future.
